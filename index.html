Do I really, really need a car in Los Angeles?

We are moving to Los Angeles, and any time I think about it, my mind conjures up:

  1. The scene of people dancing on a twisting freeway junction in La La Land
  2. The fuzzy, choking traffic Michael Douglas carved through in Falling Down
  3. The orange horizon of infinite sprawl I saw from an airplane window as we visited the city last December

We are coming from Hong Kong, which consistently ranks among the best public transport systems in the world. It’s so good, people who live in Hong Kong island or Kowloon often find a 30-minute trip intolerable on most days.

So, on top of dreading the culture shock of returning home after more than a decade away, on top of facing the prospect of being responsible for a car for the first time since I was in college, I am grappling with setting up camp in the middle of a vast place that by all appearances cannot function without private vehicles, like, at all.

But is it really that bad? Well, lets take a look, because by this measure, it can seem like you’d be just fine without a car:

This is an outline of the Los Angeles Metro system. There are four light rail lines and two rapid transit lines. There’s also the MetroLink commuter rail system, but we’re excluding that for reasons.

The green here represents a ‘walkshed’ of intracity public transport. That green blob is everywhere within 15 minutes of a stop. But its much more extensive than the Metro lines…

…and that’s because of this spiderweb of red: the Los Angeles Metro Busway.

The red here is beyond a fifteen minute walk, according to an algorithm run along walkable roads and pedestrian pathways from each stop.

Leaving this much red just out of reach within the boundary of Los Angeles County. It looks particularly bad in patches in the south, stretches of the coastline, and hills that frame the northern side of the city

That’s all just one part of the story, however. Ridership of these systems is not that high. While ridership saw an increase last year, it was more or less a return to pre-COVID levels.

And unlike in Hong Kong – where during most operation hours, trains on Hong Kong island arrive every three minutes – the LA Metro is infrequent and slow. This recent Wall Street Journal video (which has some very snazzy graphics of the train lines) argues that in ideal traffic conditions it can take four times longer to get to your destination than driving.

And to top it all off, Hong Kong has an incredible airport train. From touchdown to front door, I’ve had days where I’m back home in less than an hour. LA’s trains? They don’t even connect to the airport, surprisingly, though they say that will change. Maybe.

As to the why of all this? That’s an entirely different story.
